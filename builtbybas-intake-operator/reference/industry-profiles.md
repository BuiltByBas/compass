# Industry Profiles

Compass's domain brain. When an inquiry names or implies an industry, Compass loads
that vertical's profile to do three things well: understand the business enough to reframe
accurately, fire the right compliance flags, and apply the fit or restricted policy.

This is not legal advice and nothing here is proprietary. Compass flags compliance and
scopes it at the consultation; it does not advise on the law. Profiles describe how these
businesses generally operate so Compass reads the real need under the ask.

---

## Cross-industry data-type triggers (fire regardless of vertical)

Whenever an inquiry implies handling one of these, flag it in WHY and in the draft, and note
that compliant handling gets scoped at the consultation:

- Personal data of EU or California users -> GDPR / CCPA-CPRA.
- Card payments or a checkout -> PCI-DSS.
- Health or patient data -> HIPAA.
- Users under 13 -> COPPA.
- Student records -> FERPA.
- Consumer financial data -> GLBA.
- Biometrics (face, fingerprint) -> BIPA.
- Email or SMS marketing features -> CAN-SPAM, TCPA, CASL.
- Any public-facing site -> ADA / WCAG accessibility (a BuiltByBas strength to surface, not a risk).

---

## Restricted-industry policy (Bas's call, editable)

- Accept with care (build, but flag heavy compliance): Cannabis / CBD, Alcohol, lawful Adult content, lawful Firearms (FFL display-only, no online gun/ammo sales).
- Decline by policy (BuiltByBas does not take these): Gambling / betting.
- Case-by-case (build with a prominent flag, Bas makes the final go/no-go): Crypto / Web3.
- Decline on ethics (not restricted): UNLAWFUL adult (CSAM, evading 2257 records or age
  verification, non-consensual content) and UNLAWFUL firearms (online gun/ammo sales bypassing
  FFL/background checks, trafficking, no-license sales). The ethics screen catches these and always wins.

A restricted flag never silently changes the route. Compass states the policy and the
reason in WHY.

## Profiles

### Accounting / Financial Advisory / Bookkeeping

- Core business: tax, bookkeeping, audit, financial planning, or investment advice.
- Their clients: individuals and businesses, in sensitive financial relationships.
- How it runs: client onboarding and identity checks, document collection (tax docs, statements), engagement letters, secure file exchange, scheduling, billing, compliance filings.
- Data held: highly sensitive financial data, SSNs, tax records, investment information.
- Compliance: GLBA (financial privacy), IRS data-security safeguards for preparers, SEC/FINRA for registered investment advisors (advertising rules, recordkeeping), PCI, accessibility.
- Typical ask -> real need: "a website" is credibility plus secure document intake (Custom Website); "client portal" is secure tax/financial doc exchange (Client Portal, GLBA); "organize my client docs/workflow" is Data Organization + Workflow Design; investment-advisor marketing carries SEC/FINRA ad rules, flag it.
- Fit and flags: ACCEPT. Flags: GLBA/financial data (security-first), SEC/FINRA advertising if an RIA, PCI, accessibility.

### Agriculture / Farm

- **Core business:** A working farm that grows and sells agricultural product: fresh produce (fruits, vegetables, leafy greens), row/field crops, or a mixed operation that may also run a CSA, farm stand, or wholesale supply to grocers, restaurants, and packers. Revenue comes from harvest sold by weight/volume, CSA subscriptions, and direct-to-consumer markets.
- **Their clients:** Wholesale buyers and distributors, grocery and restaurant accounts, packing houses, farmers-market and farm-stand walk-ups, and CSA members (recurring seasonal subscribers). Buyers increasingly demand food-safety audit proof (GAP / FSMA) before purchasing.
- **How it runs:** Seasonal cycle of planting, irrigation, pesticide/fertilizer application, harvest, washing/packing, cold storage, and delivery. Labor is the operational core: crews are hired locally, through farm labor contractors, or via the H-2A seasonal visa program, with housing, transport, and wage tracking attached. Sales run through wholesale POs and invoices, CSA member signups with recurring billing, and cash/card at the stand. Recordkeeping spans pesticide application logs, water-test results, harvest/lot traceability, and payroll/hours per worker.
- **Data held:** Worker PII (names, SSNs, I-9 / H-2A visa and immigration documents, addresses, wage and hours records, housing assignments), payroll and tax data, CSA member contact lists and card-on-file/recurring payment data, wholesale buyer accounts and pricing, and operational records (pesticide application logs with applicator names, agricultural water test results, harvest lot/traceability records).
- **Compliance:**
  - FDA FSMA Produce Safety Rule, 21 CFR Part 112: applies if the farm grows/harvests/packs/holds covered produce and averages over $25,000/yr in produce sales; sets standards for agricultural water, worker hygiene, soil amendments, and lot traceability (https://www.fda.gov/food/food-safety-modernization-act-fsma/fsma-final-rule-produce-safety).
  - EPA Agricultural Worker Protection Standard, 40 CFR Part 170: applies because the farm uses pesticides in crop production; mandates worker training, application exclusion zones, restricted-entry intervals, decontamination supplies, and 2-year pesticide application/SDS records (https://www.epa.gov/pesticide-worker-safety/agricultural-worker-protection-standard-wps).
  - OSHA Field Sanitation Standard, 29 CFR 1928.110: applies to farms employing 11 or more hand-harvest field workers; requires toilets, potable water, and handwashing in the field (https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1928/1928.110).
  - Migrant and Seasonal Agricultural Worker Protection Act (MSPA), 29 U.S.C. ch. 20, applies if the farm uses migrant/seasonal labor or a farm labor contractor; governs wage disclosure, recordkeeping, housing, transportation, and FLC registration (https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/agriculture/mspa).
  - INA H-2A temporary agricultural worker program, 20 CFR Part 655 Subpart B, applies if the farm hires foreign seasonal labor; governs the work contract, wage (AEWR), housing, and U.S.-worker-protection conditions (https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/agriculture/h2a).
  - PCI DSS: applies only where card payments are accepted (farm stand card reader, CSA recurring card-on-file); a card-data-handling obligation, not farm-specific law (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility + a place buyers and CSA members find the farm and its seasonal availability = Custom Website.
  - "Let people sign up for our CSA / boxes online" -> recurring subscriber intake plus seasonal payment = E-Commerce (subscription/CSA).
  - "I can't keep track of my workers, hours, and the H-2A paperwork" -> labor + compliance recordkeeping in one place = Business Dashboard (or Full Operations Platform if it spans labor, harvest, and sales).
  - "I need to give buyers our food-safety / spray records" -> secure document sharing for audits = Client Portal.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags: worker PII + immigration documents (I-9/H-2A) are sensitive and must not sit in an unsecured spreadsheet; FSMA traceability and pesticide/water logs are audit artifacts that need integrity and retention, not ad-hoc storage; card-on-file CSA billing pushes scope into PCI (prefer a tokenized processor, never store PANs); seasonal labor records intersect MSPA/H-2A and should be handled, not improvised. Confirm whether the farm actually handles card data and whether labor is migrant/H-2A before scoping the compliance surface.

### Architecture Firm

- **Core business:** Licensed architectural design and documentation. Sells schematic design, design development, construction documents (stamped/sealed drawings and specs), permitting support, and construction administration for residential, commercial, institutional, and sometimes federal/defense buildings. Revenue is project fees, usually percentage-of-construction-cost or phased fixed fee, plus reimbursables.
- **Their clients:** Property owners and developers, general contractors, real estate and construction companies, municipalities and school districts, healthcare and institutional owners, and federal agencies (GSA, DoD, USACE) for government work. Buyer is typically an owner's rep, developer, or facilities/capital-projects manager.
- **How it runs:** Lead intake and qualification, then a proposal/AIA-style owner-architect agreement defining scope, fee, and phases. Work flows through schematic design, design development, construction documents, bidding, and construction administration. The shop runs on CAD/BIM (Revit, AutoCAD, ArchiCAD) with large drawing-set files, RFIs, submittals, and change orders exchanged with contractors and consultants (structural, MEP, civil). Milestone billing tied to phase completion; the licensed architect of record stamps and seals deliverables. Project records are retained for years to satisfy state board rules and statute-of-repose liability windows.
- **Data held:** Stamped construction drawings and specifications, BIM models, structural and MEP design data, site/security and life-safety layouts (egress, alarm, access points), client financial and project-budget information, owner and contractor contact data, signed contracts and insurance certificates, and on government work, Controlled Unclassified Information and potentially export-controlled (ITAR/EAR) technical drawings for sensitive or defense facilities. Card data only if they take card payments for fees/retainers.
- **Compliance:**
  - State architecture licensing board / firm Certificate of Authorization and the architect-of-record stamp-and-seal duty: practice of architecture is regulated jurisdiction by jurisdiction, with each state board issuing licenses, regulating firm practice, and setting record-retention obligations (https://www.ncarb.org/get-licensed/state-licensing-boards).
  - NIST SP 800-171 / CMMC 2.0 Level 2 and DFARS 252.204-7012: required only when the firm handles Controlled Unclassified Information on federal/DoD projects (drawings, schematics, technical data) (https://www.atomuscyber.com/post/cmmc-for-architectural-engineering-and-construction-companies).
  - ITAR (22 CFR 120-130): applies only if drawings/technical data are export-controlled, e.g. defense or sensitive military facility design (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-22/chapter-I/subchapter-M).
  - State consumer privacy law (e.g. California CCPA/CPRA): applies only when the firm meets the for-profit thresholds and handles personal information of residents of an enacting state (https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa).
  - PCI DSS: applies only if the firm accepts card payments for fees or retainers (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/).
  - Note: HIPAA, FERPA, and GLBA do NOT generically apply to an architecture firm. Designing a hospital or school does not make the firm a HIPAA covered entity, a FERPA-bound institution, or a financial institution. Omitted unless a specific engagement establishes that status.
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility, a portfolio of completed projects, and a qualified-lead intake funnel for owners/developers = Custom Website.
  - "A client portal" -> secure exchange of large drawing sets, RFIs, submittals, and contracts with owners and contractors = Client Portal.
  - "Something to manage all our projects" -> phase tracking, milestone billing, RFI/submittal logs, and consultant coordination in one place = Business Dashboard or Full Operations Platform.
  - "Organize our files / drawings" -> structured project archive with retention rules tied to board and liability requirements = Data Organization.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags to surface: (1) confirm whether any federal/DoD work means CUI/ITAR data will touch what we build, which forces CMMC/NIST 800-171 controls and a GovCloud-class environment, not a standard portal; (2) large BIM/drawing-set files drive storage, transfer, and portal sizing; (3) record-retention rules are state-board and liability driven, so any archive/dashboard must support long retention and controlled disposal; (4) the stamp-and-seal of the architect of record is a legal act, so our tools support workflow but must never imply we generate or alter sealed deliverables; (5) confirm card-payment handling before scoping any billing feature (PCI).

### Auto Towing

- **Core business:** Light, medium, and heavy-duty vehicle recovery and transport. Three revenue streams that operate very differently: consent tows (a customer or their insurer/motor-club calls for a breakdown, accident, or move), non-consent tows (private-property trespass, abandoned-vehicle, and police-rotation tows ordered by a property owner or law enforcement, not the vehicle owner), and impound storage (per-day storage of the towed vehicle until it is claimed and paid for, escalating to a lien sale if unclaimed).
- **Their clients:** Two split audiences that must not be conflated. Payers/dispatchers: motor clubs (AAA, Agero, Honk), insurers, fleet and dealership accounts, law-enforcement rotation lists, and property managers/HOAs who authorize trespass tows. End "customers": stranded motorists and, in non-consent cases, vehicle owners who did not choose the service and are often disputing the charge at the counter.
- **How it runs:** Call/dispatch intake (phone, motor-club digital dispatch, or roadside app) captures location, vehicle, and authorization. A driver is routed to the scene; for non-consent tows the operator must record who authorized it (name, contact, date/time) and photograph the vehicle in place. The vehicle is hooked, transported, and logged into a storage lot. Billing happens at release: tow fee plus accrued daily storage, paid before the vehicle leaves, usually by card at the gate. Unclaimed vehicles trigger a statutory notice-and-lien-sale clock (certified mail to owner and lienholder, DMV record lookup, public auction).
- **Data held:** Vehicle plate/VIN and DMV-sourced registered-owner and lienholder names and addresses (pulled to send legal notices), the caller's location and contact info, accident/police-report references, photos of the vehicle and damage, driver/employee CDL and DOT qualification files, and card payment data captured at release. For motor-club and insurer accounts they also hold claim and policy references.
- **Compliance:**
  - Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations and USDOT Number registration: tow trucks with a GVWR/GCWR of 10,001 lbs or more, or operating in interstate commerce, are commercial motor vehicles subject to FMCSRs (driver qualification files, Hours of Service, vehicle inspection/maintenance, and a USDOT Number) (https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/registration/do-i-need-usdot-number).
  - Commercial Driver's License (49 CFR Part 383): tow operators need a Class A or Class B CDL based on the combined and towed-vehicle weight ratings (https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/registration/commercial-drivers-license/do-tow-truck-operators-need-cdls-if-so-what-vehicle-groups).
  - Driver's Privacy Protection Act, 18 U.S.C. 2721-2725: governs the operator's lookup and use of DMV registered-owner/lienholder personal information. Sending notice to the owner of a towed or impounded vehicle is a permissible use, but the data may not be used or disclosed for any other purpose, and misuse carries civil liability (https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/2721).
  - PCI DSS: applies wherever the operator accepts card payment for tow and storage fees at release; merchants of any size handling cardholder data must comply (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/merchants/).
  - State towing licensure, non-consent rate caps, and abandoned-vehicle lien-sale procedure: non-consent towing, maximum allowable rates, signage/authorization rules, and the certified-mail notice-then-lien-sale process to dispose of unclaimed vehicles are set at the state/county level and vary by jurisdiction; these are statutory, not optional (representative authority: https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=46.55&full=true).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website so people can find us and call when they're stranded" -> 24/7 credibility, click-to-call, and service-area/dispatch visibility = Custom Website.
  - "Customers and police can never see where their car is or what they owe" -> authenticated vehicle-status and release-fee lookup so owners and authorizing parties self-serve = Client Portal.
  - "Dispatch is all phone calls and a paper whiteboard; drivers lose tickets" -> digitize intake, dispatch, driver assignment, and the storage/lien clock = Business Dashboard (or Full Operations Platform if billing, motor-club account reconciliation, and lien-sale tracking are in scope).
  - "Our impound notices and lien sales are a manual certified-mail mess" -> structure the abandoned-vehicle notice workflow with deadline tracking and document generation = Workflow Design.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags to surface: (1) DPPA exposure, the business pulls and stores DMV owner data, so any portal or dashboard must restrict that data to permissible-use roles and log access, never expose it to the public side. (2) Non-consent / dispute friction, the end user is frequently an angry, non-paying vehicle owner; public-facing copy and the release-payment flow must be neutral, transparent on rates, and dispute-aware, not salesy. (3) Card-at-the-gate means PCI scope, push to a tokenized processor, never store PAN in the app. (4) Verify whether the operator is on a police rotation list, that adds jurisdiction-specific rate and record-keeping rules. (5) State-specific lien-sale deadlines are load-bearing, do not hard-code one state's clock into a multi-state tool.

### Automotive (Dealership / Repair / Detailing)

- Core business: vehicle sales and/or service.
- Their clients: vehicle owners and buyers.
- How it runs: inventory (dealers), service scheduling, quotes and estimates, parts, financing (dealers), CRM, reviews.
- Data held: customer PII, financing and credit data (dealers, sensitive), payment data.
- Compliance: dealers carry GLBA (financing data), credit/lending rules (Reg Z), FTC auto-advertising rules; PCI; accessibility.
- Typical ask -> real need: "a website" is inventory plus leads (dealers) or services plus booking (repair); "manage service/inventory" is Dashboard or Workflow; financing data demands security.
- Fit and flags: ACCEPT. Flags (dealers): GLBA/credit data, auto-advertising rules, PCI, accessibility.

### Catering

- **Core business:** Off-premise food and beverage service: a kitchen (commissary or commercial) that prepares menus for events (weddings, corporate functions, private parties, funerals) and transports, holds, and serves that food at a venue the caterer does not own. Revenue is per-event, quoted from a custom proposal, not a fixed retail menu.
- **Their clients:** Couples and families booking social events, corporate event planners and office managers, venues and wedding planners who subcontract food, and institutions (schools, nonprofits, hospitals) ordering staffed meal service. Often a referral- and date-driven pipeline where the calendar slot is the scarce inventory.
- **How it runs:** Inbound inquiry with an event date, headcount, and budget -> tasting/consultation -> custom proposal and contract -> deposit to hold the date -> final headcount and menu lock a week out -> prep in a licensed kitchen, cold-chain transport, on-site holding and plating, staffing (servers, bartenders) -> final invoice and tip-out. The whole model hinges on date holds, deposits, guest counts, and dietary/allergen requirements collected per event.
- **Data held:** Client contact and event details, guest headcounts and sometimes guest dietary restrictions and allergies (health-adjacent personal data), venue addresses and access details, signed contracts, deposit and final-payment card data, staff scheduling and payroll info, and supplier/cost data. The sensitive concentrations are stored payment cards and guest allergen records.
- **Compliance:**
  - FDA Food Code, adopted and enforced by state and local health departments, governs safe handling, cold-chain holding, and time/temperature control for catered and off-premise food and is the model code that explicitly covers catering operations (https://www.fda.gov/food/retail-food-protection/fda-food-code).
  - The 2022 FDA Food Code extends allergen-notification requirements to unpackaged foods sold by caterers, requiring written disclosure of major allergens on menus, websites, or placards (https://www.fda.gov/food/fda-food-code/food-code-2022).
  - Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA) defines the nine major allergens that must be identified for packaged/labeled food the caterer offers (https://www.fda.gov/food/food-allergensgluten-free-guidance-documents-regulatory-information/food-allergen-labeling-and-consumer-protection-act-2004-falcpa).
  - State and local health-department food service permits and Certified Food Protection Manager (ANSI-accredited, e.g. ServSafe) requirements apply per jurisdiction; FDA maintains the state-by-state code index (https://www.fda.gov/food/fda-food-code/state-retail-and-food-service-codes-and-regulations-state).
  - State Alcoholic Beverage Control caterer permits apply only where the caterer serves alcohol off-site (e.g. California Type 58 catering authorization), typically with per-event authorization and server certification (https://www.abc.ca.gov/licensing/license-forms/caterers-permit/).
  - PCI DSS applies to the storage and processing of deposit and final-payment card data the caterer accepts (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/).
  - CAN-SPAM Act governs the promotional email lists and seasonal/booking marketing caterers send to past and prospective clients (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility, menu/gallery showcase, and an inquiry form that captures event date + headcount + budget = Custom Website.
  - "An online menu / quote form" -> structured event intake that filters tire-kickers and feeds the calendar = Custom Website with lead-capture (not e-commerce).
  - "Let clients order and pay online" -> deposit collection and package selection with card handling = E-Commerce (drags in PCI DSS).
  - "A system to manage my events" -> date holds, headcounts, dietary notes, staffing, and invoicing in one place = Business Dashboard or Full Operations Platform.
  - "A place for clients to see their contract and details" -> per-event secure document and proposal access = Client Portal.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags: confirm whether the build collects payments (PCI DSS scope) or only routes inquiries; surface allergen/dietary data as health-adjacent personal data that needs careful handling and display; if alcohol service is in scope, flag state ABC caterer-permit dependency (out of build scope, client's responsibility); food-safety/health-permit compliance is the client's operational duty, not the website's. No restricted-vertical or ethics concern.

### Childcare / Daycare / Preschool

- Core business: care and early education for children.
- Their clients: parents; the children are minors.
- How it runs: enrollment and waitlists, attendance and check-in, tuition billing, parent communication, ratios and licensing compliance, incident reporting.
- Data held: children's data (minors), parent PII and payment, sometimes health/allergy data.
- Compliance: COPPA if children use anything online, state licensing and ratios, staff background-check records, accessibility, PCI, high-sensitivity minor data.
- Typical ask -> real need: "a website" is info plus enrollment or tour requests (Custom Website); "a parent portal" is check-in, billing, communication (Client Portal); "manage enrollment/attendance" is Dashboard or Workflow.
- Fit and flags: ACCEPT. Flags: minors' data (high sensitivity), COPPA, state licensing, PCI, accessibility.

### Chiropractic

- **Core business:** A licensed chiropractor (solo or small group clinic) selling hands-on spinal and joint care: manual adjustments/spinal manipulation, exam and re-exam, plus adjuncts like therapeutic exercise, soft-tissue/manual therapy, traction, and sometimes X-ray, massage, or rehab. Revenue is per-visit and per-plan-of-care, mixing insurance reimbursement with self-pay/cash packages.
- **Their clients:** Local patients with back/neck pain, headaches, sciatica, sports and work injuries, and auto-accident (personal-injury) cases; wellness/maintenance regulars; referrals from PCPs, attorneys (PI), and word-of-mouth. Payer mix spans private insurance, Medicare Part B, workers' comp, auto/PIP, and out-of-pocket.
- **How it runs:** New-patient intake (history forms, complaint, consent) -> exam and often imaging -> diagnosis (subluxation/region) -> documented plan of care -> recurring visits booked in a practice-management/EHR system (ChiroTouch, Jane, Genesis, etc.). Each encounter generates a SOAP note tied to the exact spinal region treated; coding (98940/98941/98942 with the AT modifier for active care) drives electronic claim submission and eligibility checks. Front desk collects copays/deductibles and self-pay package payments by card; statements and denials are worked in the back office.
- **Data held:** Full PHI: names, DOB, SSN, addresses, insurance/member IDs, clinical histories, diagnoses, exam findings, X-ray images, treatment notes, accident/injury details, attorney and case info for PI matters, workers'-comp claim data, and stored payment-card data for recurring/self-pay billing.
- **Compliance:**
  - HIPAA Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules apply in full: a chiropractor is a covered entity once they transmit any standard electronic transaction (claims, eligibility, remittance), which virtually all billing practices do; size and solo status grant no exemption (https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/covered-entities/index.html).
  - HIPAA Security Rule: required administrative, physical, and technical safeguards plus a documented security risk analysis covering the EHR, billing system, email, backups, and devices that store or transmit ePHI (https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/laws-regulations/index.html).
  - Medicare Part B coverage limits and documentation: only manual manipulation to correct a documented subluxation is covered, encounter-specific notes and correct region coding (AT modifier; 98940-98942) are mandatory, and maintenance therapy is non-covered, an area with one of Medicare's highest improper-payment rates and direct False Claims Act / OIG audit exposure for upcoding or billing maintenance as active care (https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/chiropractic-services).
  - PCI DSS: any practice accepting payment cards for copays, deductibles, or self-pay packages is a merchant and must meet PCI DSS; HIPAA compliance does not substitute for it (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility, service/insurance clarity, and new-patient intake capture = Custom Website (with HIPAA-aware intake forms, not freeform PHI by email).
  - "Let patients book online" -> scheduling tied to existing EHR/PM and new-vs-existing patient routing = Custom Website booking integration, not a standalone PHI store.
  - "A patient portal for forms and records" -> secure intake, consent, and document exchange = Client Portal (encrypted, access-controlled, BAA in place).
  - "Our front desk and billing are a mess" -> standardize intake, scheduling, coding/claims, and denial follow-up = Workflow Design (and possibly Data Organization), explicitly NOT a clinical/coding system that practices medicine or bills on the patient's behalf.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags to surface: (1) PHI is in scope, require a signed Business Associate Agreement and keep PHI out of plain email and unencrypted forms; (2) any website intake must not become an unsecured PHI inbox; (3) stay out of clinical coding/medical-necessity decisions and Medicare billing logic, that is the practice's regulated responsibility (False Claims/OIG risk); (4) card handling routes to a PCI-compliant processor, never custom card storage; (5) confirm card-on-file/recurring billing uses a tokenizing gateway.

### Coffee Roaster / Cafe

- **Core business:** Sources green coffee, roasts it in-house, and sells it two ways: brewed drinks and food over a cafe counter, and packaged whole-bean / ground bags retail (in-store, wholesale to other cafes/grocers, and often direct-to-consumer online, including subscriptions).
- **Their clients:** Walk-in cafe customers (foot traffic, regulars, commuters); wholesale accounts (restaurants, offices, grocers buying bags or kegs); and online retail/subscription buyers who want beans shipped.
- **How it runs:** Green-bean inventory and roast scheduling drive production; roasted lots are bagged, lot-coded, and split between the retail shelf, wholesale fulfillment, and the webstore. Front of house runs a POS for drinks/food with card and mobile payments, tip handling, and often a loyalty program. Wholesale runs on standing orders, invoices/net terms, and delivery routes. Online runs a storefront with recurring subscription billing and shipping. Staff scheduling, food-handler certification, and health-inspection readiness sit underneath it all.
- **Data held:** Customer card payment data (in-store POS, online checkout, stored subscription cards); customer names, emails, shipping addresses, and order history; loyalty/marketing email list; wholesale account contacts and billing terms; employee records (scheduling, payroll, food-handler/manager certs); supplier and lot-traceability records. No protected health information, no education records, no financial-institution data.
- **Compliance:**
  - FDA food facility registration and FSMA Preventive Controls for Human Food (21 CFR Part 117) apply because the roaster manufactures/processes and holds a food product; registration must be renewed biennially and a written food safety plan maintained by a PCQI (https://www.fda.gov/food/guidance-regulation-food-and-dietary-supplements/registration-food-facilities-and-other-submissions).
  - State/local retail food code (each state's adoption of the FDA Food Code) and the local health department food-establishment permit apply because the cafe prepares and serves food and beverages to the public, requiring permitting, inspection, and a certified food-safety manager (https://www.fda.gov/food/fda-food-code/state-retail-and-food-service-codes-and-regulations-state).
  - FD&C Act and Fair Packaging and Labeling Act food labeling rules (21 CFR Part 101) apply to retail bagged coffee: statement of identity, net quantity, name/place of business, plus an ingredient list, allergen statement, and Nutrition Facts when flavorings or additives are present (https://www.fda.gov/food/food-labeling-nutrition/food-labeling-requirements).
  - FDA Menu Labeling (21 CFR 101.11) applies ONLY IF the business is part of a chain of 20 or more locations operating under the same name with substantially the same menu; do not assume this unless confirmed (https://www.fda.gov/food/nutrition-food-labeling-and-critical-foods/menu-labeling-requirements).
  - PCI DSS applies because card payments are accepted at the POS and online; most cafes are Level 4 merchants and validate via SAQ A when card data is fully outsourced to a compliant processor (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/merchants/).
  - ADA Title III applies because the cafe is a place of public accommodation and its ordering/menu website is integrated with the physical location; DOJ guidance points to WCAG 2.1 AA for the web presence (https://www.ada.gov/resources/web-guidance/).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility plus a place to show the menu, hours, story, and wholesale inquiry intake = Custom Website.
  - "I want to sell our beans online / set up subscriptions" -> storefront with recurring billing, shipping, and inventory tied to roast lots = E-Commerce.
  - "I need to manage my wholesale accounts and orders" -> standing orders, invoicing, delivery routing, and account records in one place = Business Dashboard (or CRM System if relationship/pipeline-led).
  - "Can you help me get my roasting/inventory under control" -> green-bean tracking, roast scheduling, lot traceability = Workflow Design / Data Organization.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT.   - Seal/signature and responsible-charge requirements enforced by the state licensing board; affixing a seal certifies the work was prepared by or under the licensee's direct supervision, carrying personal and firm professional liability (https://www.nspe.org/sites/default/files/resources/pdfs/GR/NSPE_COPA_Stamp_Doc.pdf).

Flags: (1) the PE seal is a hard liability boundary, COMPASS routes the digital/ops deliverable and never touches engineering judgment, stamping, or anything that implies practicing engineering; (2) confirm whether the work touches defense/aerospace or critical-infrastructure data before any portal/file-handling build, because ITAR/EAR can make a foreign-national developer or offshore hosting a violation, route to PREREQUISITE if export-control status is unstated; (3) a Client Portal handling sealed documents needs real access control, versioning, and retention, scope it as a build, not a brochure site; (4) no PHI/FERPA/GLBA/PCI here unless a specific payment or records function is explicitly added, do not flag them by default.

- **Their clients:** Grieving next-of-kin and families arranging an at-need funeral, often within 24-72 hours and under acute emotional load; pre-need purchasers (typically older adults or estate planners) arranging in advance; and institutional referrers who feed cases in: hospitals, hospices, nursing homes/assisted living, hospice social workers, coroners/medical examiners, churches, and insurance/pre-need agents.

Pre-need runs a parallel track: a contract is sold and the money is placed in a state-regulated trust or funded by a life insurance policy, then redeemed years later at the time of death.

- **Data held:** Decedent identity and vital-statistics data (full SSN, DOB/DOD, place of death, parents' names, military service records/DD-214); cause-of-death and medical/health information received from the hospital, hospice, or attending physician (including infectious-disease and organ/tissue-donation status relevant to embalming); next-of-kin contact and family relationship details; payment data (credit cards, insurance policy/assignment numbers, bank info for pre-need trusts); signed itemized statements and pre-need contracts; and body chain-of-custody and prep-room records.

- **Compliance:**
  - FTC Funeral Rule, 16 CFR Part 453 - directly governs this vertical: requires a printed General Price List given to keep, itemized written Statement of Funeral Goods and Services Selected, a Casket Price List and Outer Burial Container Price List, telephone price disclosure, and bans tying/required-purchase practices (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-453).
  - HIPAA Privacy Rule decedent provisions, 45 CFR 164.512(g) and 164.502(f) - the funeral home is generally NOT a covered entity, but it RECEIVES protected health information disclosed by hospitals/hospices to carry out its duties, and a decedent's PHI stays protected for 50 years after death, so any system holding that PHI must safeguard it (and a Business Associate Agreement is required if the home contracts to handle a covered entity's PHI) (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-45/subtitle-A/subchapter-C/part-164/subpart-E/section-164.512).
  - OSHA Formaldehyde standard, 29 CFR 1910.1048 - embalming exposes workers to formaldehyde; mandates exposure monitoring, PEL/action-level controls, and training (https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1910/1910.1048).
  - OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens standard, 29 CFR 1910.1030 - prep-room contact with blood/OPIM; requires a written Exposure Control Plan, Hepatitis B vaccination offer, and PPE (https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1910/1910.1030).
  - OSHA Hazard Communication standard, 29 CFR 1910.1200 - embalming fluids/disinfectants require labeling, Safety Data Sheets, and chemical training (https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1910/1910.1200).
  - State funeral establishment + funeral director/embalmer licensing and pre-need/prepaid funeral trust laws - establishment and practitioner licenses are mandatory and pre-need funds must sit in a regulated trust or insurance vehicle; rules are state-specific (representative: Texas Funeral Service Commission establishment licensing, https://www.tfsc.texas.gov/establishmentlicensing.html).
  - PCI DSS - applies because the home accepts card payments at-need and for pre-need; it is a card-brand contractual standard, not a government regulation, governing storage/transmission of cardholder data (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/).
  - EPA / local POTW pretreatment for embalming wastewater and formaldehyde-waste disposal - prep-room effluent and chemical waste are regulated under Clean Water Act pretreatment standards and local sewer-authority limits (https://www.epa.gov/npdes/national-pretreatment-program).

- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "We need a website / our site looks dated" -> credibility, 24/7 first-call visibility, obituaries, and online arrangement intake for grieving families = **Custom Website** (or **Website Redesign**).
  - "Can families plan/pay online?" -> secure pre-need arrangement + payment intake, not a brochure = **Landing Page** or **E-Commerce** scoped to pre-need with PCI-compliant payment, NOT card data stored in-house.
  - "We want families to upload documents / sign and access paperwork securely" -> secure document exchange for death certificates, contracts, and PHI-adjacent records = **Client Portal**.
  - "Our case management is a mess of paper and spreadsheets" -> at-need/pre-need case tracking, removal-to-disposition chain-of-custody, permit/death-certificate status, and trust accounting = **Business Dashboard** or **Full Operations Platform** (and likely **Data Organization** + **Workflow Design** for the current paper process).

- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags to surface: (1) decedent PHI received from hospitals/hospices means any portal or database must encrypt and access-control that data and may require a BAA; (2) never store raw card or full SSN data in a custom app, push payments to a PCI-compliant processor and tokenize; (3) pre-need money is regulated trust/insurance money, the platform records contracts but must not become an unlicensed handler of those funds; (4) obituaries and arrangement flows touch grieving users, so accessibility, tone, and reliability (the first-call path can never be down) are non-negotiable; (5) FTC Funeral Rule price-disclosure obligations mean published pricing on the site must be accurate and complete.

Tooling is typically AutoCAD/SketchUp/Revit or Chief Architect for drawings, a design-business platform (DesignFiles, Ivy/Houzz Pro, Studio Designer, Mydoma) for proposals/invoices/procurement, QuickBooks for books, and Stripe/Square/ACH for client payments. Vendor relationships and trade pricing are core IP.

**Data held**
Client contact and project records; home addresses, access details, alarm codes, and photos of the interior of clients' homes (a real privacy/physical-security concern); budgets and financial capacity; signed contracts and e-signatures; payment data (cards via a processor, ACH/bank details, deposits/retainers held); vendor trade-account credentials and confidential trade pricing; floor plans and proprietary design files. Commercial projects add building/tenant data and life-safety documentation.

**Compliance** (each line backed by an applicable, sourced regulation)
- State title/practice acts regulate the profession: three states plus DC and Puerto Rico restrict the *practice* of commercial interior design (FL, LA, NV), while many other states protect the *title* ("Registered/Certified Interior Designer"). Applicability depends on the firm's state and whether it does commercial work, confirm per jurisdiction. (https://www.cidq.org/for-certificate-holders/jurisdictions-and-requirements/)
- ADA Standards for Accessible Design (2010) apply to any new construction or alteration of places of public accommodation and commercial facilities, directly binding on commercial/contract interior designers' space planning, clearances, restrooms, and signage. Does NOT apply to private single-family residential work. (https://www.ada.gov/law-and-regs/design-standards/)
- FTC Cooling-Off Rule (16 CFR Part 429) gives consumers a 3-business-day right to cancel sales of $25+ made at the buyer's home or away from the seller's permanent place of business, squarely relevant when a designer signs a residential client at the client's home and takes a retainer. (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-429)
- PCI DSS applies whenever the firm accepts card payments for deposits/retainers/invoices. Using a hosted processor (Stripe/Square) keeps scope to the lowest tier, but the obligation is triggered by accepting cards at all. (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/)
- CAN-SPAM Act governs the firm's commercial/marketing email (newsletters, portfolio promos): valid sender info, honest subject lines, working opt-out. Applies to essentially every US firm that emails prospects. (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business)
- CCPA/CPRA applies only if the for-profit firm meets a threshold: $25M+ gross annual revenue (inflation-adjusted to ~$26.6M for 2026), OR buys/sells/shares personal info of 100,000+ California consumers/households, OR derives 50%+ of revenue from selling personal info. Most boutique design firms fall BELOW these thresholds and are not covered, flag only if the firm is large or sells client data. (https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa)

Note on omitted regulations: HIPAA, FERPA, and GLBA do NOT apply to a standard interior design firm, it handles no PHI, no education records, and is not a financial institution. The one edge case is a designer doing a *healthcare facility* build-out who is given patient-area data by a covered-entity client; even then the obligation flows through a Business Associate relationship, not from being a designer. Do not flag these absent that specific fact.

**Typical ask → real need** (2-4 mappings)
- "I want a beautiful new website / portfolio site" → Custom Website / Website Redesign. Real need: a portfolio-led, fast, mobile-first site that converts referral traffic into booked consultations, with strong project galleries and a qualified-lead intake form. (BUILD: Custom Website / Website Redesign / Landing Page)
- "I'm drowning in spreadsheets, proposals, invoices, and vendor POs" → real need is a procurement + project + billing workflow, not a website. Map to a Business Dashboard / CRM and/or Workflow Design rather than rebuilding what Studio Designer/Houzz Pro already do, reframe if they're describing an off-the-shelf design-business platform. (BUILD: Business Dashboard / CRM System; OPS: Workflow Design)
- "Clients should be able to see their project, approve selections, and pay" → Client Portal with proposal approval, e-sign, and milestone payments. Real need: structured client communication and on-time payment, with PCI scope kept on the processor. (BUILD: Client Portal)
- "I want to sell my product picks / curated pieces online" → E-Commerce, but verify trade-account/markup terms permit resale and that payment + sales-tax handling is real before committing. (BUILD: E-Commerce)

**Fit and flags**
ACCEPT. Interior design is a clean, low-regulatory-risk services vertical for website, portfolio, client-portal, CRM, and workflow work. ACCEPT WITH CARE only when: (a) the firm handles card/ACH data directly rather than through a hosted processor (PCI scope), (b) the build stores home addresses, access codes, or interior photos, treat as sensitive and design for least-exposure, or (c) the firm is large enough to cross CCPA thresholds or sells client data. No DECLINE basis exists for the vertical itself.

### IT Services / MSP

**Core business**
A managed service provider (MSP) sells ongoing, outsourced IT operations under a recurring contract (monthly per-seat or per-device retainer) rather than break/fix project work. Typical delivery stack: remote monitoring and management (RMM) agents on every client endpoint and server, a professional services automation (PSA) ticketing/billing platform, remote access tooling, patch and backup management, endpoint detection and response (EDR), and a help desk. Revenue concentrates in managed endpoints, managed Microsoft 365/Google Workspace tenants, managed firewalls/networks, cloud and backup hosting, and increasingly co-managed security (vCISO, SOC). The defining trait for routing: an MSP holds privileged, persistent administrative access into many other businesses at once, so it is both a vendor and a single point of systemic risk.

**Their clients**
Small and mid-sized businesses across mixed verticals (professional services, healthcare practices, accounting/finance, legal, dealerships, manufacturing, nonprofits) and sometimes defense-supply-chain firms. The MSP rarely owns the regulated data itself, it administers the systems that hold its clients' regulated data. That inheritance is the whole compliance story below: an MSP picks up its clients' regulatory obligations by contract through Business Associate Agreements, service-provider clauses, and flow-down terms.

**How it runs**
RMM agents and admin credentials give the MSP standing remote access to client endpoints, servers, hypervisors, and SaaS admin consoles. A PSA system stores client contacts, asset inventories, network documentation, credentials/passwords (often in an integrated vault), tickets, and contracts. Backups and disaster-recovery copies of client data frequently sit in MSP-managed cloud or local appliances. Work is delivered by a help desk and field/remote technicians, often with offshore or subcontracted labor. The MSP is itself a high-value attack target precisely because compromising one MSP can cascade into every downstream client (the well-documented supply-chain pattern behind Kaseya/SolarStorm-class incidents).

**Data held**
Client network and infrastructure documentation, administrative and service-account credentials, password vaults, full backups and DR images of client systems (which can contain anything the client stores), endpoint telemetry and logs, PSA records (client PII, contracts, billing). Where clients are regulated, the MSP's backups, vaults, and remote sessions touch that client's PHI, cardholder data, CUI/FCI, or nonpublic financial information even when the MSP never opens the files.

**Compliance**
- FTC Safeguards Rule, 16 CFR Part 314 (GLBA implementing rule). Applies when the MSP serves financial-institution clients (CPAs, mortgage brokers, auto dealers, tax preparers, finance companies) as a "service provider" that maintains or accesses customer information, and increasingly when the MSP itself acts as the client's outsourced Qualified Individual. Section 314.4(f) requires those clients to contractually obligate the MSP to implement and maintain safeguards, so the obligation flows down to the MSP. (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-314) (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-safeguards-rule-what-your-business-needs-know)
- HIPAA Security Rule and Business Associate provisions, 45 CFR 160.103 and 45 CFR 164.504(e). Applies ONLY when the MSP creates, receives, maintains, or transmits PHI for a healthcare covered entity, which an MSP running backups, mailboxes, or remote support for a medical/dental practice routinely does. That makes the MSP a Business Associate requiring a signed BAA; storage/backup of PHI counts even if the MSP never views it (the conduit exception covers transmission only, not persistent backup storage). (https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/privacy/guidance/business-associates/index.html)
- CMMC Program, 32 CFR Part 170. Applies ONLY when the MSP processes, stores, or transmits Federal Contract Information or Controlled Unclassified Information for a DoD-contractor client, making the MSP an External Service Provider. Note: the final rule imposes no standalone CMMC certification mandate on MSPs; rather, when CUI flows to the MSP its services are pulled into the client's CMMC assessment scope. Does not apply to MSPs with no defense-supply-chain clients. (https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/10/15/2024-22905/cybersecurity-maturity-model-certification-cmmc-program)
- State data breach notification laws (all 50 states), e.g. New York SHIELD Act and the NCSL multistate survey. Applies broadly: most statutes reach any entity that owns OR merely "maintains" residents' personal information, which squarely covers an MSP holding client backups and PSA data. Note: where the MSP only maintains (does not own/license) the data, statutes like SHIELD direct it to promptly notify the owning client, who then carries consumer/AG notification. (https://www.ncsl.org/technology-and-communication/security-breach-notification-laws)
- PCI DSS v4.0.1 (card-brand contractual standard, not a federal statute), Requirement 12.8 / TPSP obligations. Applies ONLY where the MSP hosts, accesses, or could affect a client's cardholder data environment (hosting a merchant's POS network, managing a CDE firewall). Triggers written TPSP agreements (12.8.2) and annual AOC monitoring (12.8.4); does not apply to MSPs that never touch card data. (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/)

OMITTED (do not flag): FERPA unless the MSP serves a school/education-records client; SOX, FINRA, NERC CIP, GDPR/state privacy acts unless a specific in-scope client relationship exists. None are inherent to being an MSP.

**Typical ask -> real need**
- "We need someone to manage our IT / monitor our systems" -> ongoing managed services retainer (Full Operations Platform or Business Dashboard if they want a unified ops/visibility layer). This is the MSP's own service line, not a build, route accordingly.
- "Build us a client portal / dashboard so clients can see tickets, assets, and SLAs" -> Client Portal or Business Dashboard build on top of their existing PSA/RMM data. Real need is a presentation/self-service layer, confirm read-only vs write integration to the PSA.
- "We're drowning in spreadsheets for asset inventory and onboarding" -> Data Organization plus Workflow Design (OPS), not a custom app. Real need is structured process before tooling.
- "Help us pass our client's HIPAA/CMMC/Safeguards audit" -> this is regulated compliance consulting, not a website/app build. Real need is a security-program engagement; if it sits outside the offered BUILD/OPS tracks, route to PREREQUISITE (missing facts: which client, which framework, what evidence exists) or REFER.

**Fit and flags**
ACCEPT WITH CARE. MSP-facing build/ops work (portals, dashboards, asset organization, onboarding workflow) is well within scope and a natural fit. The care flags: (1) any integration that reads PSA/RMM data inherits whatever regulated data those systems hold, so confirm whether PHI, CUI, cardholder, or financial data is in scope before architecting; (2) credentials, password vaults, and backup data are radioactive, never request or store them, design for least-privilege, scoped API access only; (3) "manage our IT" and "pass our audit" asks are services/consulting, not deliverables in the build/ops catalog, route them out rather than forcing a build. DECLINE only on ethics or genuine non-fit, not for the regulated nature alone.

### Law Firm / Legal

- Core business: sells legal expertise, advising and representing clients, drafting documents, handling disputes or transactions. Practice area (family, criminal, estate, corporate, IP, personal injury, real estate) changes everything about their needs.
- Their clients: individuals or businesses in high-stakes, stressful situations. Discretion is part of the product.
- How it runs: client intake and conflict-of-interest checks, matter/case management, document drafting and version control, deadline and statute-of-limitations calendaring (a missed date is malpractice), hourly time tracking and billing, trust/IOLTA accounting, secure communication, court filings, e-discovery.
- Data held: highly confidential client information, privileged attorney-client communications, case files, trust-account data, sometimes clients' medical or financial records.
- Compliance: attorney-client privilege and confidentiality (security-first), state bar advertising rules (limits on "specialist," guarantees, testimonials), trust-accounting rules, breach obligations, ADA accessibility, HIPAA if handling medical records.
- Typical ask -> real need: "a website" is a credibility site plus secure intake (Custom Website); "a client portal" is secure document exchange plus matter status and invoices (Client Portal, security non-negotiable); "help us manage cases" is matter-management workflow (Workflow Design -> Business Dashboard); marketing refers to Marketing partner, flag the bar advertising rules.
- Fit and flags: ACCEPT. Flags: confidentiality/privilege, bar-advertising limits on copy, trust accounting if billing is in scope, accessibility.

### Locksmith

- **Core business:** Mobile and storefront lock-and-key services: residential/commercial/automotive lockouts, rekeying, lock installation and repair, key cutting and duplication, transponder/fob programming, safe opening, and access-control or master-key system setup. Revenue is mostly per-job dispatch with a smaller storefront/retail line.
- **Their clients:** Homeowners and renters (lockouts, rekeys after a move or break-in), small-business and property managers (commercial rekeys, master-key systems, access control), landlords doing tenant turnover, drivers locked out of vehicles, and the occasional insurance/roadside-assistance referral.
- **How it runs:** Inbound call or web form -> dispatcher captures address, job type, and ID for proof-of-ownership -> a licensed tech is routed to site -> tech verifies the caller actually owns/controls the property or vehicle (a legal duty in licensed states) before defeating a lock -> work performed -> on-site card/mobile payment and a documented invoice listing license number, tech, and the authorization captured. Books are run from a CRM/dispatch tool plus accounting; warranty and call-back history matter because the job is literally "I let you into a secured space."
- **Data held:** Customer name, service address (often a home), phone/email, driver's license or other ID collected to verify ownership before entry, vehicle VIN/plate and key/transponder codes for auto work, lock and key codes / master-key bitting / access-control credentials for properties, and stored payment card data. This is high-sensitivity security data: a leak is a literal physical-security exposure (who lives where, and how to get in).
- **Compliance:**
  - State locksmith licensing, bonding, insurance, and proof-of-ownership/recordkeeping duties where required (13 states plus local jurisdictions); e.g. Texas regulates locksmiths under the DPS Private Security Board with licensing, background checks, and customer-verification/recordkeeping rules (https://www.dps.texas.gov/section/private-security/licensing-and-registration).
  - PCI DSS, because they accept Visa/Mastercard/Amex/Discover on-site and in-shop; smallest merchants are Level 4 and self-attest via SAQ, but the standard still applies (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/merchants/).
  - State data-breach notification laws (all 50 states), triggered because they hold name combined with driver's license number, payment card data, and security/access codes (https://www.ncsl.org/technology-and-communication/security-breach-notification-laws).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website so people can find us and call" -> local credibility, trust signals (license #, bond, reviews), and click-to-call/booking = **Custom Website** (or **Landing Page** if single-service/ads-driven).
  - "Customers should book and pay online" -> intake + dispatch-ready scheduling with deposit capture = **Custom Website** with booking, or **Business Dashboard** if they also want job tracking.
  - "We're drowning in calls and lose jobs" -> the real need is dispatch/job routing and tech assignment, not marketing = **Business Dashboard** / **Workflow Design**.
  - "We need to keep customer and key-code records straight" -> secure record-of-service with ID/authorization and key/access codes = **Client Portal** or **CRM System** (encrypted, access-controlled).
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags: (1) they hold physical-security data (addresses + access/key codes), any portal or CRM must be encrypted with strict access control and audit logging, this is the load-bearing risk. (2) Confirm state licensing/bonding status before publishing any "licensed locksmith" credibility claims on a site we build. (3) If card payments touch our system, keep them PCI-scoped via a hosted/tokenized processor (Stripe/Square), do not store PANs. (4) Driver's-license capture for proof-of-ownership pulls them into state breach-notification scope; minimize/retain only what licensing requires.

### Logistics / Freight

- Core business: Moves goods for hire and arranges that movement. Spans asset-based motor carriers (own trucks/drivers), freight brokers (match shippers to carriers, never touch the freight), freight forwarders (consolidate, issue bills of lading, assume cargo liability), and 3PLs that blend all three plus warehousing and drayage. Revenue is the margin between what the shipper pays and what the carrier is paid, or the linehaul/accessorial rate on owned equipment.
- Their clients: Shippers (manufacturers, distributors, retailers, e-commerce sellers) needing capacity, and on the supply side a roster of carriers and owner-operators. For forwarders and customs work, importers/exporters of record. Demand is lane-driven and seasonal; relationships hinge on on-time performance and claims handling.
- How it runs: Load comes in by phone/email/EDI/load board (DAT, Truckstock) -> rate quote and margin set -> carrier sourced and vetted (authority, insurance, safety score) -> rate confirmation and dispatch -> pickup, in-transit tracking (ELD/telematics/check calls), POD capture at delivery -> invoice the shipper, pay the carrier (often via quick-pay/factoring), reconcile accessorials and detention, and manage OS&D/cargo claims. Carrier onboarding (W-9, authority, certificate of insurance, carrier packet) and credit/collections run in parallel.
- Data held: Carrier authority records and FMCSA safety/insurance data, driver names and CDL details, signed rate confirmations and bills of lading, PODs, shipper and carrier bank/ACH and factoring details, EINs and W-9s, customs/commercial-invoice and HS-code data for cross-border loads, GPS/ELD location traces, and pricing/margin data that is competitively sensitive. Where the business runs its own fleet: driver DOT drug-and-alcohol testing and Clearinghouse records, hours-of-service logs, and qualification files.
- Compliance: Broker and freight-forwarder operating authority and registration through FMCSA's Unified Registration System, with BOC-3 process-agent and insurance filings (https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/registration/getting-started); broker/forwarder financial responsibility, the $75,000 BMC-84 surety bond or BMC-85 trust under 49 CFR 387.307 (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-III/subchapter-B/part-387/subpart-C/section-387.307); for any owned-fleet operation, FMCSA hours-of-service and ELD rules under 49 CFR Part 395 (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-III/subchapter-B/part-395) and controlled-substance/alcohol testing plus the CDL Drug and Alcohol Clearinghouse under 49 CFR Part 382 (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-III/subchapter-B/part-382); hazardous-materials handling under the PHMSA Hazardous Materials Regulations, 49 CFR Parts 171-180, where any hazmat is offered or transported (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-171); for cross-border/import work, customs-broker licensing and conduct under 19 CFR Part 111 and CBP's ACE filing environment (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-111). PCI DSS applies only if the platform itself captures card payments rather than ACH/factoring (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/).
- Typical ask -> real need: "We need a website with instant quotes" -> credibility front door plus a structured load/quote intake that feeds dispatch, real need is a Custom Website with a quote-request flow, not a public rating engine. "A carrier/customer portal" -> secure exchange of rate cons, PODs, COIs and invoices with role separation, real need is a Client Portal. "A dashboard to see all our loads" -> live load board, margin, on-time and aging-AR across reps, real need is a Business Dashboard. "Connect our load board, TMS and QuickBooks so we stop double-entering" -> intake-to-settlement automation across carrier onboarding, tracking and invoicing, real need is a Full Operations Platform.
- Fit and flags: ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags to surface: confirm operating authority and bond status before building public-facing rating or carrier-pay features (regulated, money-movement adjacent); keep carrier banking/ACH and factoring data encrypted and access-scoped (financial PII); if cross-border or hazmat is in scope, customs (19 CFR 111) and PHMSA (49 CFR 171-180) obligations attach and should not be designed around; carrier-vetting features must read FMCSA authority/insurance as source of truth, never substitute for it; if the platform handles card payments, PCI scope is triggered, steer to ACH/tokenized rails to avoid it.

### Management Consulting

- **Core business:** Sells expertise and judgment, not a product. A firm is engaged to diagnose a client's business problem (strategy, operations, org design, M&A diligence, finance/cost transformation, change management, IT and digital transformation) and deliver recommendations, often with implementation support. Revenue is project fees, retainers, or time-and-materials against scoped engagements (SOWs).

- **Their clients:** Other businesses and institutions, not consumers. Mid-market through enterprise, plus private equity, government agencies, healthcare systems, and financial institutions. The buyer is usually a C-suite or functional leader (CEO, CFO, COO, CHRO) sponsoring a specific initiative.

- **How it runs:** Inbound lead or referral, then a scoping/discovery call to define the problem; a written proposal/SOW with fees, deliverables, and an MSA + NDA. Staffing a partner plus an analyst/manager team. Engagement work is heavy on client data ingestion: the firm pulls financials, HR rosters, customer lists, system exports, and interviews staff. Deliverables are decks, models, and roadmaps in a shared workspace. Billing is milestone or monthly invoice; many firms run on time-tracking and utilization. Subcontractors and offshore analyst pods are common, which extends the data footprint.

- **Data held:** Highly confidential third-party business data that is more sensitive than the firm's own: client strategic plans, unannounced M&A targets and deal terms, financial statements and forecasts, pricing and margin data, customer and prospect lists, and full employee datasets (names, SSNs, comp, performance) when doing HR/org work. Where the client is regulated, the firm also ingests that client's regulated data: patient records (healthcare clients), cardholder and account data (financial clients), and controlled technical data (defense/aerospace clients). Plus its own pipeline CRM and staff PII.

- **Compliance:**
  - FTC Act Section 5 (unfair or deceptive acts or practices) is the baseline federal data-security standard for any US business holding personal information, requiring "reasonable" security and honoring of the firm's own privacy/security promises (https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statutes/federal-trade-commission-act).
  - State data breach notification laws apply in all 50 states; a consulting firm holding another company's PII is typically the data processor and must notify the data owner, and may itself notify individuals, within statutory deadlines (https://iapp.org/resources/article/state-data-breach-notification-chart/).
  - State comprehensive privacy laws (e.g., California CCPA/CPRA) reach the firm as a "service provider" or "contractor" when it processes personal information on a client's behalf; the B2B and employee exemptions expired Jan 1, 2023, so the firm must sign compliant data-processing terms and provide CCPA-equivalent protection (https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa).
  - HIPAA applies only when a healthcare-sector client gives the firm access to Protected Health Information; the consultant is then a "business associate," is directly liable, and must execute a Business Associate Agreement (https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/privacy/guidance/business-associates/index.html).
  - GLBA / FTC Safeguards Rule applies only when the firm receives nonpublic personal financial information from or for a financial-institution client and is acting as a service provider to that institution's information-security program (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-safeguards-rule-what-your-business-needs-know).
  - ITAR / EAR export controls apply only when a defense, aerospace, or dual-use client shares controlled technical data; releasing it to foreign-national staff is a "deemed export" requiring a technology control plan (https://www.pmddtc.state.gov/ddtc_public/ddtc_public?id=ddtc_kb_article_page&sys_id=24d528fddbfc930044f9ff621f961987).

- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "We need a website / refreshed site" -> credibility and inbound lead capture for a referral-driven firm = **Custom Website** (intake + case studies + partner bios).
  - "A secure portal to share deliverables with clients" -> NDA-bound document exchange replacing email attachments and consumer file-share = **Client Portal**.
  - "A dashboard to see project status and utilization" -> consolidate time tracking, staffing, and margin per engagement = **Business Dashboard**.
  - "Organize all our engagement files / templates" -> the firm's own knowledge and document sprawl across drives = **Data Organization**.
  - "Standardize how we run an engagement" -> repeatable intake-to-delivery process = **Workflow Design**.

- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. The firm itself is unregulated, but it acts as a custodian of other companies' most sensitive data, so any system that touches deliverables or client data must assume signed NDAs/MSAs and contractual security duties. FLAGS: (1) confirm whether engagements involve PHI (HIPAA BAA), financial-institution data (GLBA/Safeguards), or controlled technical data (ITAR/EAR) before any portal or storage build, and route those as their own compliance scope; (2) a "client portal" holding M&A or deal data raises access-control and audit-logging stakes; (3) subcontractor/offshore staff access can trigger deemed-export or data-processing-agreement obligations; (4) avoid building anything that stores client regulated data without the matching agreement in place.

### Manufacturing / Industrial / B2B

- Core business: producing and supplying goods to businesses.
- Their clients: business buyers and distributors.
- How it runs: product catalogs and spec sheets, quotes and RFQs, orders, inventory or ERP, distributor portals, long sales cycles.
- Data held: business and order data, pricing, sometimes export-controlled technical data (defense/aerospace).

### Marketing / Creative Agency

- **Core business:** A services firm that plans and produces marketing for other businesses: brand identity, websites and landing pages, ad creative (copy, design, video, motion), social and influencer campaigns, email and SMS programs, SEO/content, and paid-media buying. Revenue is project fees and monthly retainers; the deliverable is creative work plus the campaigns that run it.
- **Their clients:** Other businesses (B2B). SMB owners, marketing managers, and founders who lack an in-house team, plus larger brands offloading overflow or specialist work. The agency rarely sells to end consumers itself; it markets TO consumers ON BEHALF OF clients.
- **How it runs:** Lead comes in (referral, inbound, RFP) -> discovery/scoping call -> proposal and signed SOW or retainer agreement -> creative brief and kickoff -> production in rounds with client review/approval -> campaign launch on the client's owned channels and ad platforms (Meta, Google, TikTok, email/SMS ESPs) -> reporting dashboards -> invoicing (deposit + milestones, or monthly retainer). The agency is handed access to the client's ad accounts, CMS, analytics, and frequently the client's customer/contact lists to run email and SMS sends.
- **Data held:** Client business credentials and platform access (ad-account, CMS, ESP, analytics logins/tokens); the client's customer and prospect contact lists (names, emails, phone numbers, purchase/behavior data) uploaded for email/SMS and custom-audience targeting; campaign performance and conversion data tied to identifiable consumers; creative assets, brand IP, unpublished product launches and pricing under NDA; influencer/contractor PII and payment details.
- **Compliance:**
  - FTC Act Section 5 prohibition on unfair or deceptive acts and the advertising-substantiation doctrine: every objective ad claim the agency authors must have a reasonable basis, and the agency can be held liable for deceptive ads it creates or disseminates (https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/45).
  - FTC Guides Concerning Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising, 16 CFR Part 255: agencies, PR firms, and intermediaries are expressly liable for influencer, testimonial, and review content that lacks clear-and-conspicuous disclosure of a material connection (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-255).
  - CAN-SPAM Act and implementing rule, 16 CFR Part 316: commercial email the agency sends on a client's behalf needs accurate headers, a non-deceptive subject, a valid physical postal address, and opt-outs honored within 10 business days; the sender and the party promoted can both be liable (https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-316).

### Property Inspection

**Core business**
A property inspection firm (residential home inspectors and small commercial inspection shops) is hired to physically examine a building from foundation to roof and produce a written, opinion-bearing report on its condition: structure, roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, foundation, moisture, and safety defects. The deliverable is the report, usually time-boxed inside a real estate transaction (a 7-to-10-day inspection contingency window). Many shops upsell ancillary services that materially change their data and compliance footprint: radon, mold, termite/WDO, sewer scope, well/septic, thermal imaging, and drone roof inspection. Revenue is per-inspection fees; the operator is typically a 1-to-10 person business, often owner-operated.

**Their clients**
Primarily home buyers (the inspection client, who owns the report and the contractual relationship), driven heavily by referrals from real estate agents who maintain "preferred inspector" lists. Secondary clients: sellers (pre-listing inspections), home owners, landlords/property managers, investors, and relocation companies. The agent relationship is the dominant lead channel, which makes referral tracking and fast scheduling commercially critical.

**How it runs**
Lead comes in (agent referral, web form, call), inspector confirms scope and books a slot, performs the on-site inspection (2-4 hours), captures photos and notes on a tablet/phone in the field, and generates a narrative + photo report (often via dedicated inspection software like Spectora, HomeGauge, or ISN). Client pays before or at report release; report is delivered as a PDF/web link. Back office handles scheduling, agreement e-signature, payment, calendar/route optimization for multi-job days, and review requests. Continuing education and license renewal are recurring obligations where the state regulates the trade.

**Data held**
Client PII (name, email, phone, billing address), the subject property address and detailed condition findings with geotagged photos, signed pre-inspection agreements (limitation-of-liability contracts), payment data, agent/referral source records, and scheduling/calendar data. With ancillary services they may hold lab results (radon, mold, water) and drone imagery. No PHI, no education records, no financial-account data of the consumer. The sensitive asset is the report itself: it carries legal liability and is sometimes disputed, so retention and integrity matter more than volume.

**Compliance**
- State home inspector licensing and standards of practice: the trade is regulated at the state level (roughly 35+ states license inspectors; there is no federal license or national mandate). Licensed states set pre-licensing education hours, exams (commonly the NHIE), supervised-inspection requirements, continuing education, and bonding/insurance. A web/CRM that stores license numbers, CE credits, and the signed inspection agreement must respect these state rules ([InterNACHI state licensing](https://www.nachi.org/licensing-and-certification), [ASHI state regulations](https://www.homeinspector.org/resources/state-regulations/), [NHIE state regulations](https://nationalhomeinspectorexam.org/stateregulations/)).
- ASHI / InterNACHI Standards of Practice and Code of Ethics: not a statute, but courts and many state laws treat these as the standard of care a report is measured against, so report structure and disclaimers must align ([InterNACHI Standards of Practice](https://www.nachi.org/sop.htm)).
- EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Rule, Section 1018 of Title X (42 U.S.C. 4852d): applies to pre-1978 target housing; if the inspector performs lead inspection/risk assessment they must be EPA/state certified under TSCA and follow prescribed work practices. Only triggered when lead services or pre-1978 stock are involved ([EPA Section 1018 disclosure rule](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule-section-1018-title-x), [EPA lead inspection certification](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-abatement-inspection-and-risk-assessment)).
- FAA Part 107 (14 CFR Part 107): any drone roof/aerial inspection flown for compensation requires a certificated remote pilot and compliance with Part 107 operating rules. Only applies if the firm uses drones commercially ([14 CFR Part 107](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-107), [FAA become a drone pilot](https://www.faa.gov/uas/commercial_operators/become_a_drone_pilot)).
- PCI DSS: applies because most inspectors collect card payment online or in the field at or before report release; any payment surface we build must tokenize and never store raw card data ([PCI DSS standard](https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/)).

**Typical ask -> real need**
- "I need a website with online booking" -> a scheduling system tied to inspector availability and service area, agreement e-signature, and pre-payment capture, not just a brochure site (Custom Website + Business Dashboard).
- "Build me a client portal so buyers and agents can get their report" -> secure, access-controlled report delivery with per-party permissions (buyer vs agent), versioning, and an audit trail given the liability of the document (Client Portal).
- "I'm drowning in scheduling and agent referrals" -> workflow + data organization around lead source, routing/calendar, and referral attribution back to agents (Workflow Design + Data Organization / CRM System).
- "I want to manage everything in one place" -> consolidated ops platform: jobs, payments, license/CE tracking, report status, and review requests (Full Operations Platform).

**Fit and flags**
ACCEPT. Standard small-business build territory: scheduling, portal, payments, CRM, ops dashboard. Care points rather than blockers: (1) report delivery is the legally sensitive asset, so access control, integrity, and retention must be deliberate; (2) payment surfaces are PCI scope, use a tokenizing processor and never touch raw PAN; (3) do not build features that imply the firm performs lead, drone, or other regulated ancillary services unless they actually hold EPA/FAA/state credentials, the platform should record the credential rather than assert compliance on their behalf. No HIPAA, FERPA, or GLBA: this vertical holds no PHI, education records, or consumer financial-account data.

### Recruiting / Staffing Agency

- **Core business:** Sources, screens, and places candidates into roles for client companies. Two revenue models: direct/permanent placement (one-time fee on hire) and temp/contract staffing, where the agency is the legal employer-of-record, runs the worker on its own W-2 payroll, and bills the client a marked-up hourly rate.
- **Their clients:** Two-sided. On the demand side, hiring managers and HR at client employers (SMB through enterprise, often concentrated in verticals like IT, healthcare, light industrial, finance, admin). On the supply side, the candidates/job seekers being placed. The candidate is the product; the employer pays.
- **How it runs:** Intake of a client job order (title, pay band, must-haves) -> sourcing from ATS/CRM, job boards, and referrals -> screening calls and skills/qualification checks -> candidate consent and background/drug screening via a third-party CRA -> client interviews -> offer. For temp/contract: onboarding the placed worker onto agency payroll (Form I-9, W-4, direct deposit), weekly timesheet collection, payroll run with tax withholding, client invoicing, and managing assignment start/end. Permanent placement ends at the guarantee-period invoice.
- **Data held:** Candidate PII at scale: full legal name, SSN/SIN, date of birth, home address, phone, personal email, government ID and work-authorization documents (driver's license, passport, visa/EAD), resumes and full work history, references, compensation history and pay-rate expectations, bank/direct-deposit details for placed workers, tax withholding (W-4) data, and consumer/background-check and drug-screen results. On the client side: confidential job orders, pay bands, and hiring-manager contacts.
- **Compliance:**
  - Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) governs every background check ordered through a consumer reporting agency: standalone written disclosure, prior written authorization, and the two-step pre-adverse / final adverse action process before declining a candidate on report contents (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/what-employment-background-screening-companies-need-know-about-fair-credit-reporting-act).
  - Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, enforced by the EEOC, expressly covers employment agencies and bars discriminatory screening, referral, or candidate filtering on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin (https://www.eeoc.gov/laws/guidance/policy-guidance-what-constitutes-employment-agency-under-title-vii-how-should-charges) and applies the EEOC's limits on how background information may be used in hiring decisions (https://www.eeoc.gov/laws/guidance/background-checks-what-employers-need-know).
  - Immigration Reform and Control Act / Form I-9: when the agency places a worker on its own payroll it is the employer and must complete and retain Form I-9 verifying employment eligibility within three business days of hire (https://www.uscis.gov/i-9).
  - Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) worker-classification and joint-employer rules: the agency must correctly classify placed workers as employees vs. independent contractors under the DOL economic-reality test, and may be jointly and severally liable with the client for wages and overtime as a joint employer (https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa/misclassification).
  - State data-breach notification law (e.g., California Civil Code 1798.82): the SSN- and ID-heavy candidate file triggers mandatory consumer notice on breach of unencrypted personal information, with SSN breaches requiring offered identity-theft mitigation (https://codes.findlaw.com/ca/civil-code/civ-sect-1798-82/).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "We need a website so candidates and clients take us seriously" -> credibility plus a structured intake funnel for job orders and applicants = Custom Website.
  - "A portal where candidates upload resumes and ID docs" -> secure, access-controlled handling of SSNs, work-authorization documents, and consent records = Client Portal.
  - "Our recruiters live in spreadsheets and lose track of candidates" -> a pipeline/ATS-style system tracking candidates, job orders, submissions, and placements = CRM System (trending toward Full Operations Platform once payroll/timesheet/invoicing are in scope).
  - "Can you connect our job board, timesheets, and invoicing?" -> staged automation of the temp-staffing back office = Workflow Design (likely MULTI-PHASE).
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. This vertical holds bulk SSNs, government IDs, work-authorization documents, and bank details, so any build touching candidate data must enforce encryption at rest/in transit, role-based access, audit logging, retention/disposal aligned to FCRA and I-9 retention rules, and explicit consent capture. Flags to surface: FCRA disclosure/authorization and adverse-action workflow must be designed in (not bolted on); EEOC exposure if any automated filtering/screening logic could create disparate impact (especially AI resume-ranking); breach-notification readiness given the data class; clarify whether they are the employer-of-record (payroll/I-9 obligations) or pure permanent-placement. We build the operational and data infrastructure; we do not provide legal/regulatory advice, and the agency remains responsible for its FCRA, EEOC, and I-9 compliance posture.

  - National Electrical Code (NFPA 70) and utility interconnection standards IEEE 1547 / UL 1741 SB: the technical/safety code the install and grid-tied inverters must meet to pass inspection and earn permission-to-operate (https://www.nfpa.org/codes-and-standards/nfpa-70-standard-development/70).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "We need a website" -> credibility for in-home/canvassing leads plus a lead-capture and instant-quote intake = **Custom Website** (often with a savings-estimate intake form).
  - "A customer portal / app" -> homeowners want to see production, financing balance, and project status securely (PII + financing data) = **Client Portal**.
  - "Something to track our jobs" -> pipeline from lead to PTO across sales, permitting, install, interconnection = **CRM System** or **Full Operations Platform**.
  - "Online financing / sign-up flow" -> collecting credit and loan-disclosure data in-app = surfaces TILA + Cooling-Off compliance, route as **Client Portal / Custom Website** WITH CARE, not a generic form.
- **Fit and flags:** **ACCEPT WITH CARE.** Buildable vertical (website, portal, CRM, ops platform), but two flags must be surfaced: (1) any sales or sign-up flow must preserve the 16 CFR 429 right-to-cancel disclosure and not engineer around it; (2) any financing/credit-application capture pulls TILA/Reg Z and CFPB UDAAP exposure (dealer-fee transparency, tax-credit claims), so credit data needs proper handling and the installer's lender relationship defines liability. Do not build features that obscure cancellation rights or loan cost.

### Tax Preparation (seasonal)

- **Core business:** A small, owner-operated tax preparation practice (often an EA, CPA, or unenrolled preparer with a PTIN) that prepares and e-files individual and small-business income tax returns for compensation. Revenue is highly concentrated in the Jan-Apr filing season, with extensions, amended returns, IRS notices, and light bookkeeping filling the off-season. Many operate as a side practice or a seasonal pop-up storefront.

- **Their clients:** Individual filers (W-2 wage earners, gig/1099 workers, retirees), self-employed and single-member LLCs, small partnerships/S-corps, landlords, and recent-immigrant or mixed-status households. Largely repeat clients who return year over year, plus a seasonal surge of walk-ins and referrals.

- **How it runs:** Intake by appointment or walk-in during season; client drops off or uploads source documents (W-2s, 1099s, 1098s, K-1s, prior-year returns, IDs, SSN cards). Preparer keys data into professional tax software (Drake, ProSeries, Lacerte, UltraTax), reviews, gets the client to sign Form 8879 (e-file authorization), then transmits to the IRS/state via the preparer's EFIN. Billing is per-return or flat fee, often with bank-product/refund-transfer options. Off-season is notice resolution, amendments, and records retention. The pain points are seasonal document chaos, insecure email/text exchange of SSNs, signature collection, and status-chasing.

- **Data held:** Full taxpayer PII at the highest sensitivity tier: SSNs/ITINs for filers and every dependent, dates of birth, full income and asset detail, bank account/routing numbers for direct deposit, scanned government IDs, prior-year returns, employer and payroll data, and IRS-issued IP PINs. This is exactly the "Federal Tax Information" (FTI) and customer nonpublic personal information (NPI) that data-theft rings target.

- **Compliance:**
  - IRC Section 7216 and 26 CFR 301.7216 -- criminal/civil restriction on a preparer's disclosure or use of tax return information without specific written client consent; misdemeanor up to one year and fines, plus a separate civil penalty under IRC 6713 (https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/301.7216-1)
  - FTC Safeguards Rule, 16 CFR Part 314 (implementing the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act) -- paid tax preparers are "financial institutions" and must maintain a written information security program (WISP) appropriate to the data they hold (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-safeguards-rule-what-your-business-needs-know)
  - FTC Safeguards Rule breach-notification amendment (effective May 13, 2024) -- notify the FTC no later than 30 days after discovering a security event involving the unencrypted information of 500 or more consumers (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/blog/2024/05/safeguards-rule-notification-requirement-now-effect)
  - IRS Publication 4557, Safeguarding Taxpayer Data -- the IRS directive requiring every preparer to have a written data security plan and implement the "Security Six" controls; ties the WISP obligation to the GLBA (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p4557.pdf)
  - IRS Publication 1345 (Handbook for Authorized IRS e-file Providers) -- e-file program rules: protect the EFIN, secure transmission (TLS), Form 8879 signature authorization, and report data theft to the IRS Stakeholder Liaison (https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p1345.pdf)
  - IRS PTIN requirement -- every individual who prepares returns for compensation must hold and annually renew a valid Preparer Tax Identification Number (https://www.irs.gov/tax-professionals/ptin-requirements-for-tax-return-preparers)

- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website so people can find me and book" -> credibility plus a secure intake/booking funnel that does NOT collect SSNs in a plain form = **Custom Website** with a vetted scheduling/intake step.
  - "Let clients upload their tax docs to me" -> encrypted, access-controlled document exchange replacing email/text of SSNs (a GLBA/Pub 4557 obligation, not a convenience) = **Client Portal**.
  - "A dashboard to see where every return is" -> return-status pipeline across the season (received -> in prep -> review -> 8879 signed -> e-filed -> accepted) = **Business Dashboard**.
  - "Organize my client files / stop the document chaos" -> structured, retention-aware records system and naming/intake discipline = **Data Organization** (OPS), often paired with **Workflow Design** for the seasonal surge.

- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. This vertical holds maximum-sensitivity PII (SSNs, bank info, government IDs) and sits squarely under GLBA/FTC Safeguards plus IRC 7216 criminal exposure. Flags: (1) never build a plain web form that captures SSNs or bank details, route to an encrypted portal; (2) any client-data system must support the preparer's WISP (encryption at rest and in transit, MFA, access logging, retention) per Pub 4557 and the Security Six; (3) confirm IRC 7216 written-consent boundaries before any feature that reuses or shares return data (marketing, analytics, third-party integrations); (4) build breach-detection/logging so a 500-consumer event can be identified and reported within the FTC 30-day window; (5) verify the preparer holds a valid PTIN/EFIN before standing up anything that touches e-file workflow.

### Trade / Professional Association

Sponsors and exhibitors are a third buyer at events.

Governance runs on a board, committees, and member voting/elections. Advocacy runs as a government-affairs team filing lobbying reports and, where present, a separate PAC. Finance runs dues + event + sponsorship receivables, and an annual Form 990 filing.

  - Annual Form 990 information return is required under IRC 6033, and IRC 6104 requires the org to make its 990s and exemption application publicly available on request (https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i990) (https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/6104).
  - IRC 6033(e) requires the association to either notify members of the non-deductible (lobbying) share of their dues or pay a proxy tax on lobbying expenditures (https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/other-non-profits/business-leagues).
  - Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 requires registration and periodic lobbying reports once federal-lobbying time/expense thresholds are met, which advocacy-driven associations routinely cross (https://lobbyingdisclosure.house.gov/lda.html).
  - Sherman Act / FTC antitrust rules govern the signature risk of this vertical: members are competitors, so information-sharing, standard-setting, benchmarking, and membership-restriction activities must avoid price/output coordination (https://www.justice.gov/atr/antitrust-issues-and-your-small-business/participating-information-sharing-and-trade-associations).
  - FEC rules apply if the association operates a PAC (separate segregated fund): solicitation is limited to the restricted class, with registration and contribution reporting (https://www.fec.gov/help-candidates-and-committees/fundraising-for-ssf/solicitable-class-trade-association-ssf/).
  - PCI DSS applies wherever dues, event registration, or certification fees are paid by card; the AMS/payment vendor must be PCI-validated or the association is non-compliant (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/standards/pci-dss/).
  - CAN-SPAM governs commercial member/prospect email (opt-out, accurate headers, physical address) (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business).
  - TCPA governs membership/event SMS and autodialed calls (prior express written consent for marketing texts, honor opt-out) (https://www.fcc.gov/sites/default/files/tcpa-rules.pdf).

- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "We need a new website" -> public credibility + member self-service join/renew + standards/cert lookup = **Custom Website** fronting the AMS, not a brochure site.
  - "A member portal / login area" -> gated member resources, dues status, certification transcript, document library = **Client Portal**.
  - "Online dues and event registration / a store" -> recurring dues billing, event tickets, exhibitor sales with stored payment = **E-Commerce** (PCI-scoped).
  - "A members database / move off spreadsheets" -> unified roster, renewals, committees, certification tracking, reporting = **CRM System** or **Business Dashboard** depending on whether they want records management or KPI visibility.

- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flags: confirm whether they want us to own payment flows (PCI scope -> keep card data with a validated processor, never in our DB); the member portal holds real PII and certification records so treat it as a secure system, not a marketing page; any feature that publishes or shares member-submitted industry data is an antitrust-sensitive surface (aggregate only, third-party-blinded) and is a legal/policy call for the client, not a UI decision; lobbying/PAC pages and Form 990 publishing are public-disclosure features, not custom logic we invent.   - PCI DSS: applies because the planner stores card-on-file and processes deposits/installments; even when a processor (Stripe/Square/Honeybook) handles the card, the merchant is responsible for SAQ-level compliance (https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org/document_library/).
  - CAN-SPAM Act: applies to commercial email marketing to leads and past couples: accurate headers, physical address, clear and honored opt-out within 10 business days (https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business).
  - TCPA: applies to promotional SMS/calls to prospects and clients: prior express written consent, 8am-9pm local-time window, honor opt-outs (FCC tightened opt-out handling April 2025) (https://www.fcc.gov/sites/default/files/tcpa-rules.pdf).
  - State sales / occupancy / event tax registration: planning services, rentals, and bundled vendor charges are taxable in many states; multi-jurisdiction events trigger registration where the event occurs (https://www.taxadmin.org/state-tax-agencies).
  - NOTE: HIPAA, FERPA, and GLBA do not apply (no PHI, no education records, not a financial institution). CCPA/CPRA generally does not apply to a typical small planner, which falls under the $25M-revenue / 100k-consumer / 50%-data-sale thresholds; flag only if the business crosses one (https://oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa).
- **Typical ask -> real need:**
  - "I need a website" -> credibility, portfolio gallery, and a qualified-lead intake form that captures date/budget/guest count = Custom Website.
  - "A client portal for my couples" -> secure document/contract sharing, timelines, and guest-list collaboration = Client Portal.
  - "Something to keep my bookings and vendors straight" -> pipeline, contracts, deposits, vendor and task tracking in one place = CRM System or Business Dashboard.
  - "Take deposits / payments online" -> PCI-aware checkout and installment billing on the booking flow = E-Commerce (payments) scoped into the Custom Website or Portal.
- **Fit and flags:** ACCEPT. Flags to surface: (1) PCI scope: keep card data on a compliant processor, never in app DB or spreadsheets; (2) consent capture, intake forms and SMS/email nurture need explicit opt-in language for TCPA/CAN-SPAM; (3) client-funds handling, if the planner holds deposits in trust before paying vendors, contracts and bookkeeping must reflect it; (4) watch for marketing-only asks (SEO, ads, social growth) which are a REFER or SPLIT SCOPE, not a build.

## Restricted profiles

### Cannabis / CBD (accept with care)

- Core business: cultivation, dispensary, or CBD product sales.
- Why care: state-by-state legality and a federal gray area, severe payment-processing restrictions, strict advertising rules (ad platforms ban it), mandatory age-gating.
- Compass action: ACCEPT WITH CARE. The build proceeds (Custom Website, or E-commerce with caveats), but flag heavily: age verification, no standard card processing (specialized processors only), advertising restrictions (a Marketing partner marketing referral is constrained, flag that), and state compliance. Confirm their state's legality at the consultation.

### Alcohol / Brewery / Winery / Spirits (accept with care)

- Core business: producing or selling alcohol.
- Why care: age-gating, direct-to-consumer shipping laws that vary by state, advertising rules, the three-tier distribution system.
- Compass action: ACCEPT WITH CARE. Flag: age verification, DTC shipping compliance if they sell online (E-commerce with a shipping-law caveat), advertising rules.

### Gambling / Betting / Gaming (decline by policy)

- Core business: real-money wagering, betting, or gaming as the business itself.
- Why decline: heavy licensing, age and geo restrictions, payment and legal complexity, a jurisdiction minefield.
- Compass action: DECLINE (restricted). Decline politely, not a BuiltByBas fit. Note: a sweepstakes or contest used as a promotion by an otherwise normal business is fine; the decline is for gambling as the core business.

### Firearms / Weapons (accept with care, if lawful)

- Core business: a licensed firearms dealer (FFL) or range/retailer wanting a credibility or
  brochure presence (who they are, hours, services, range info, lead capture), NOT online sale of
  guns or ammunition.
- Why care: ATF/FFL regulation, advertising and payment-processor restrictions, platform ad bans,
  and a hard line at e-commerce (transferring a firearm requires an FFL and background check, so
  online gun/ammo checkout is out of scope and must route through the dealer's FFL process).
- Compass action: ACCEPT WITH CARE. A lawful FFL display-only / credibility site builds like any
  other (usually BE FOUND, or a Website Redesign), flagging: FFL/ATF compliance, NO online firearm
  or ammunition sales in scope (display and lead capture only; transfers go through the FFL), age
  and transfer law, and constrained advertising (a marketing referral carries the ad-platform
  limits). Confirm the dealer is properly licensed at the consult.
- Hard boundary: an UNLAWFUL firearms request -> DECLINE (ethics), not restricted. This covers
  online gun or ammunition sales that bypass FFL transfer and background checks, trafficking,
  no-license sales, and selling to prohibited persons. The ethics screen catches these and wins.

### Adult Content (accept with care, if lawful)

- Core business: lawful adult or explicit content and services (subscription content sites,
  cam/live-streaming, adult retail) operated by and featuring verified adults.
- Why care: mandatory age verification (state laws such as Texas HB 1181, upheld by SCOTUS
  2025-06-27, now in ~25 states), 18 U.S.C. 2257 records-keeping and a named records custodian,
  high-risk payment processing (CCBill, Segpay, not standard card rails), platform/advertising
  bans, and DMCA/content-ownership handling.
- Compass action: ACCEPT WITH CARE. The build proceeds (E-Commerce / membership site, Client
  Portal for a creator area, or a Website Redesign), but flag heavily: age verification at the
  front door, the 2257 records-custodian notice, a high-risk processor, performer-age and consent
  verification, and content-moderation duties. Confirm performers are verified adults and 2257
  compliance is in place at the consult.
- Hard boundary: an UNLAWFUL adult request -> DECLINE (ethics), not restricted. This covers CSAM
  or any minors, evading age verification or 2257, non-consensual or revenge content, and
  trafficking. The ethics screen catches these and always wins over accept-with-care.

### Crypto / Web3 / Token Projects (case-by-case, Bas decides)

- Core business: exchanges, token projects, NFT platforms, DeFi, wallets.
- Why care: tokens may be securities, money-transmission licensing, a fraud-heavy space, fast-changing regulation.
- Compass action: ROUTE the build with a prominent flag and DEFER the final go/no-go to Bas. Distinguish a normal business that merely accepts crypto payments (fine, flag lightly) from a token, exchange, or DeFi project (high regulatory risk, Bas decides). Name the securities and money-transmission risk in WHY.
